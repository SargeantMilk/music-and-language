Neuroscience

Linguistic processing in the brain relies heavily on two specific areas of the left hemisphere: Broca’s area and Wernicke’s area. If either of these two areas are damaged, for example by a head injury or stroke, language production and comprehension are likely to be negatively affected. However, this does not necessarily lead to impairment in musical ability; in particular, patients with Broca’s aphasia often have greater fluency in music than in speech (Schlaug et al., 2010).

Musical processing also has dedicated neural systems, some of which overlap or correspond to areas associated with language processing (Koelsch et al., 2004; Levitin & Menon, 2003). Congenital amusia, a condition in which perception of musical syntax is impaired, has also been shown to correspond with abnormal syntactic processing in language tasks (Sun et al., 2018). This demonstrates that certain fundamental elements of language and music share significant aspects of processing.
